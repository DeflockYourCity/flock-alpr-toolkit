## Benjamin Franklin's Quote: The Scholarly Context That Makes It Devastating

> "Those who would give up essential Liberty, to purchase a little temporary Safety, deserve neither Liberty nor Safety."
> — Pennsylvania Assembly, 1755; *The Papers of Benjamin Franklin*, Vol. 6, pp. 238-243 (Yale University Press)

### What Franklin Was Actually Arguing About

In the 1750s, the Pennsylvania Assembly (led by Franklin) was fighting the Penn family — hereditary proprietors of the colony. French and Indian War raids were devastating frontier settlers. The Assembly wanted to **tax the Penn family's lands** to fund defense. The Penns refused and instead offered a **one-time lump-sum payment** if the Assembly would surrender its power to tax proprietary lands.

- The **"essential Liberty"** was the legislature's sovereign authority to govern — specifically, the power of elected representatives to levy taxes for defense
- The **"temporary Safety"** was the Penn family's lump-sum offer — a short-term fix that would have permanently weakened the legislature's governing power

### Why This Is More Powerful Than the Bumper Sticker Version

Franklin was arguing that **elected representatives should never trade away structural governing power for a short-term safety benefit offered by a powerful private interest.**

Applied to **[REDACTED]**:
- The "essential Liberty" is the **council's meaningful oversight and control** over surveillance infrastructure
- The "temporary Safety" is the crime-reduction promise offered by a vendor whose business model depends on platform expansion
- The structural danger: approving surveillance without binding governance controls means trading permanent governing authority for a temporary assurance

**Scholarly source:** Benjamin Wittes, "What Ben Franklin Really Said" (Lawfare / Brookings, 2011)

## Founding Fathers Quotes — Ready to Deploy

### James Madison — Federalist No. 51 (1788)
> "If men were angels, no government would be necessary... you must first enable the government to control the governed; and in the next place oblige it to control itself."

**Application:** Surveillance power requires structural checks, not trust. An ALPR system with vendor-controlled settings, configurable retention, and expandable features requires the same structural constraints the Founders demanded of government itself.

### Thomas Jefferson — Letter to Edward Carrington, 1788
> "The natural progress of things is for liberty to yield, and government to gain ground."

**Application:** Jefferson identified a one-directional ratchet. Surveillance infrastructure follows this exact pattern — cameras added, rarely removed; retention extended, rarely shortened; sharing enabled, rarely disabled.

### Thomas Jefferson — Kentucky Resolutions (1798)
> "In questions of power, let no more be heard of confidence in man, but bind him down from mischief by the chains of the Constitution."

**Application:** "Bind him down from mischief by the chains of the Constitution" is the founding-era version of "put it in the contract." Jefferson explicitly rejected the idea that trust is sufficient.

### Patrick Henry — Virginia Ratifying Convention, June 9, 1788
> "The liberties of a people never were, nor ever will be, secure, when the transactions of their rulers may be concealed from them."

**Application:** Direct argument for transparency — public audit logs, disclosed sharing agreements, open reporting.

### Chief Justice Roberts — Carpenter v. United States, 585 U.S. ___ (2018)
> "A person does not surrender all Fourth Amendment protection by venturing into the public sphere... Seismic shifts in digital technology... [make it possible] to achieve near perfect surveillance."

**Application:** The Supreme Court has already recognized that mass automated location tracking implicates constitutional rights, even in public spaces.

## Texas Constitutional Context

### Article I, Section 9 (Search and Seizure)
> "The people shall be secure in their persons, houses, papers and possessions, from all unreasonable seizures or searches."

Interpreted by Texas courts as providing **independent and sometimes broader** protections than the Fourth Amendment. The Texas Court of Criminal Appeals has held in multiple cases that Art. I, Sec. 9 provides protections beyond the federal floor.

### Article I, Section 19 (Due Course of Law)
> "No citizen of this State shall be deprived of life, liberty, property, privileges or immunities... except by the due course of the law of the land."

Texas uses "due course of law" rather than "due process" — interpreted at times as broader than the federal standard.

## The "Ratchet" — Why This Is Not a Slippery Slope Fallacy

A slippery slope is a fallacy when the chain of escalation is speculative. Here, the escalation is **documented, repeated, and structurally incentivized**.

### Documented Surveillance Expansion Patterns

**ALPR → Immigration Enforcement:**
- Illinois: CBP accessed ALPR data in violation of state law (Aug 2025)
- Mountain View: Flock enabled nationwide setting without permission (Jan 2026)
- DEA: Built national ALPR database, initially justified for border drug interdiction, expanded to cover major highways nationwide (ACLU FOIA, 2015)

**Gunshot Detection → Voice Monitoring:**
- ShotSpotter: Acknowledged sensors *can* capture audio beyond gunshots (AP, 2021)
- Chicago OIG: Alerts "rarely produce evidence of a gun crime" (Aug 2021)
- Flock Raven: Gunshot detection now expanding to "human voices in distress" (EFF, Oct 2025)

**Camera Networks → Facial Recognition:**
- London: CCTV network later integrated with live facial recognition
- NYC Domain Awareness System: Post-9/11 cameras evolved into ALPR + facial recognition + predictive analytics platform
- Detroit Project Green Light: Camera program added facial recognition; resulted in **first documented wrongful arrest** from faulty FR match (Robert Williams, ACLU, June 2020)
- Clearview AI: Run against existing camera infrastructure by thousands of agencies without public disclosure (NYT, Jan 2020)

**Flock's Own Trajectory:**
1. ALPR (plates only) → Vehicle Fingerprint (characteristics) → FreeForm AI (natural language search) → Raven (audio) → National Lookup (cross-jurisdiction) → Patent describing race/gender classification

### Why It's a Ratchet, Not a Slope

- **Documented** — every example is real, not hypothetical
- **Structurally incentivized** — venture-backed companies grow by selling more features
- **Technically frictionless** — software updates expand capability without hardware, hearings, or votes
- **Historically consistent** — every major surveillance system in modern history expanded beyond its original scope

## Rhetorical Strategy Summary

1. **Lead with governance, not opposition** — "I support effective law enforcement. My concern is governance."
2. **Use the vendor's own words** — patents, marketing, product pages
3. **Cite government sources first** — audits, city statements, NVD, court rulings
4. **Ask questions, don't accuse** — puts burden on council to demonstrate due diligence
5. **Invoke shared Texas values** — bipartisan, constitutional, not partisan
6. **Pair every concern with a solution** — you're the responsible adult, not the obstructionist
7. **Use the "responsible procurement" frame** — "We wouldn't approve a water plant without an environmental assessment"
8. **Acknowledge the legitimate need** — "No one wants more crime. The question is governance."
